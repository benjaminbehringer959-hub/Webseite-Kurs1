Day 1

Day 1

Before you change anything about your phone habits, one thing matters more than any tip or rule: knowing where you are right now. Digital detox starts with honesty, not restriction. Many people only notice that they are constantly distracted but can hardly say how strong it really is or what they actually want to achieve. That is exactly where day one begins.

Today you set one single goal that directly relates to your studying and your phone. Not a huge life goal, but something concrete, measurable, and realistic for your current life. At the same time you do a short baseline check: how stable is your concentration right now, and how confident do you feel about your goal? The focus test and the self-check are not for evaluation, but a starting point.

Optionally you can record your current screen time. This is voluntary and only for you. Numbers are not a judgment, but a reference for later.

At the end of this day you have not changed anything yet. But you did something crucial: you gave your digital detox a clear direction.

Focus test

Short 5-10 minute test. Note the result for comparison later.

Tasks for today

Task 1

Formulate a personal SMART goal for your social media use. * Example:* "On school days I use social media for a maximum of 30 minutes per day."

Task 2

Start your course journal (date, goal, motivation). * Example:* "I am doing the course because I often get distracted while studying."

Task 3

Do a short focus test (5-10 minutes). * Example:* Mark multiples of 3 on a number sheet for 2 minutes.

Bonus

Screenshot or roughly estimate your screen time. * Example:* "Today about 2 hours of social media."

Fact of the day: Research on goal setting shows that specific, time-bound goals reduce decision effort and change behavior more reliably than vague intentions.

Why this works

When people notice they are on the phone too much, a strange inner pressure often appears. You know it distracts you, but you still grab it automatically. That is not weakness. It is a signal that your system needs clearer orientation.

A vague goal like "use my phone less" creates constant decisions in every situation. Clear goals reduce decision load and make behavior more stable. Measuring your baseline helps you see where you start, not to judge you.
