Day 12

Day 12

When you think back to yesterday, you may have noticed how much already happens at the beginning of the day before you "really" start. Maybe the morning was clearer, maybe just a bit calmer. Maybe you also noticed that it was less about willpower than about the frame you gave yourself.

Today you take that thought into your study time. While studying there are often quiet moments when you get stuck, do not understand a paragraph right away, or notice your thoughts begin to wander. In those seconds the phone feels especially close, not necessarily because you want to use it, but because it is available as a quick interruption.

The task today is therefore very concrete and deliberately small. You study once without the phone in sight, for a clearly limited block. The phone is in a box, a drawer, or another room. It is not about being especially focused or "pushing through," but about noticing how learning feels when this stimulus is not constantly in view.

The goal today is not performance, but an experience of distance.

Tasks for today

Task 1

Set up a fixed study place with no phone in sight. * Example:* Desk with notebook, book, pen, water - phone in another room.

Task 2

Do two phone-free study phases there (e.g., 2x45 min or 3x25 min). * Example:* 4:00-4:45 math (phone in backpack), 5:00-5:45 English.

Task 3

After each phase, briefly note if you were less distracted. * Example:* "Fewer interruptions, tasks done faster."

Bonus

Mark the study place visibly (post-it "Study time"). * Example:* Post-it "phone out of sight" on the desk.

Fact of the day: When the context is stable, getting started is often easier.

Why this works

Context triggers behavior. When the phone is in sight, the cue to check is stronger. A stable, phone-free study place reduces cue-reactivity.

A consistent study environment makes starting easier and keeps attention more stable.

Side Fact: When the context is stable, getting started is often easier.

Quiz

Question 1: What is a learning zone?

It is a stable place that supports focus.

Question 2: What does cue-reactivity mean?

Visible cues make checking more likely.

Question 3: Why keep the phone out of sight?

Fewer cues reduce automatic impulses.

Reflection

Write your thoughts for yourself only. Nothing is saved.

Question 1: How did it feel to learn without my phone in sight?

Question 2: Did I have fewer inner interruptions?

Question 3: What felt unusual?

Question 4: What felt relieving?
