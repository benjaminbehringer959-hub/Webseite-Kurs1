Day 2

Before you start today, take a quick moment and look back at yesterday. You set a goal and noted where you are. Maybe that felt surprisingly clear, maybe a little uncomfortable. If you noticed thoughts like "Okay, so this is what it really feels like," then you are exactly where you should be today.

Today is not about changing or improving anything. It is about understanding why your phone pulls your attention so easily, even when you intend to do something else. You might know this from studying: you sit down, the material is not even that hard, and still your mind drifts, often straight toward your phone.

The task today is deliberately simple. You turn off notifications for social apps for one day or use a focus mode. Not to control yourself, but to observe what happens. Every time you still feel the impulse to check, you briefly note when it happened and how it felt. At the end you add a short self-check.

The goal today is not discipline, but understanding. You do not have to push through or "be strong." You only have to notice.

Tasks for today

Task 1

Turn off all social media notifications for 24 hours. * Example:* Mute Instagram, TikTok, and Snapchat completely.

Task 2

Note every urge to check anyway. * Example:* "3:30 pm - boredom during the break."

Bonus

Short exchange with one person. * Example:* "I noticed how automatic grabbing my phone is."

Fact of the day: Variable rewards strengthen behavior patterns more strongly than predictable ones.

Why this works

The pull of the phone is not mainly about satisfaction. It is about expectation. Dopamine creates curiosity and the feeling that something important might show up. That is why unpredictable feeds are so sticky.

Notifications intensify this effect. A buzz or pop-up grabs attention even if you do not react. Turning them off does not remove the impulse immediately, but it makes the pattern visible so you can observe it.

Side Fact: Variable rewards strengthen behavior patterns more strongly than predictable ones.

Quiz

Question 1: What does dopamine do according to the bonus section?

Dopamine creates anticipation and curiosity, not satisfaction.

Question 2: How do notifications affect attention?

A buzz or pop-up pulls attention even if you do not react.

Question 3: Why is the phone often used?

The phone is often used to get brief relief, not because it is truly exciting.

Reflection

Write your thoughts for yourself only. Nothing is saved.

Question 1: In which moments did I feel the impulse to reach for my phone today?

Question 2: How quickly did I notice that impulse?

Question 3: Was there a moment when I just noticed it?

Question 4: How did the observing feel?
